Syllogistic reasoning, originating from Aristotle's work, forms the foundation of formal logic. A syllogism is a form of deductive reasoning where the conclusion is drawn from two given premises.

## Structure of a Syllogism

Every syllogism consists of:
- **Major Premise**: A general statement
- **Minor Premise**: A specific statement related to the major
- **Conclusion**: The inferred result

<CodeBlock code={`Major Premise: All humans are mortal.
Minor Premise: Socrates is human.
Conclusion: Therefore, Socrates is mortal.`} />

## Types of Syllogisms

There are four types of categorical syllogisms:

| Figure | Structure |
|--------|-----------|
| 1 | M-P, S-M → S-P |
| 2 | P-M, S-M → S-P |
| 3 | M-P, M-S → S-P |
| 4 | P-M, M-S → S-P |

<Blockquote icon="psychology">
  The validity of a syllogism depends not on its content but on its form.
</Blockquote>
