Imagine walking into a kitchen and finding flour, sugar, yeast, and eggs simply dumped on a counter. You do not have bread yet. You just have raw ingredients. Science is very much the same. A heap of facts is not a science until we apply an organized recipe to combine them.
<br/>
Look up at the night sky. You will see thousands of isolated, scattered stars. But human beings rarely settle for isolated dots of light. We draw imaginary lines between them to create familiar shapes and constellations.
<br/>
This is exactly how logic and science operate. A massive list of completely true but disconnected facts is not a science. Science is organized knowledge.
<br/>
But how exactly do we draw those logical lines? Roughly speaking, we organize knowledge by proving how some facts naturally follow from other facts. We do this by building what logicians call a deductive system.
<br/>
Okay, let's slow down here, because we very quickly hit a logical wall. Suppose you ask me for directions to the town library, and I tell you it is right next to the post office. When you ask where the post office is, I say it is right next to the library. I have given you two perfectly true facts, but you are walking in a circle. The exact same thing happens in formal logic if you try to define every single term or prove every single statement using another statement. You eventually get trapped in an endless, unhelpful loop.
<br/>
So, what does this actually mean in practice? It means we must surrender the urge to prove absolutely everything. To get our system off the ground, we have to accept a handful of starting definitions without explaining them. These are our primitive terms. We also have to accept certain baseline statements without proving them. We call these statements axioms or postulates.
<br/>
Think of axioms like the starting rules of a board game like chess. You never ask why the knight is only allowed to move in an L-shape. You simply accept that rule as your absolute starting point. From that single, unproven assumption, you can go on to build a million brilliant, complex strategies.
<br/>
For a very long time, stretching back to the ancient Greek mathematician Euclid, scholars actually believed that mathematical axioms were grand, undeniable truths about the physical universe. But modern thinkers had a massive realization. Axioms are truly just assumptions. If you discard Euclid's classic rules of geometry, you do not destroy math. You just build an entirely new, non-Euclidean system. That might sound strange, but that strange new geometry was exactly what Einstein needed centuries later to map out the weirdly warped nature of outer space.
<br/>
This brings us to a major mental pivot. In formal logic, the actual real-world truth of your starting axiom is entirely irrelevant. Logicians care almost entirely about validity. They only want to know if step B naturally follows step A in a completely unbreakable chain.
<br/>
However, everyday human language gets in the way here. If I use a word like "plane" or "line," you immediately picture something physical in your head. You might accidentally bring your own private biases or unspoken rules into our argument. To fix this, mathematicians completely abstract the entire system. They swap out familiar words for totally blank, empty symbols. By doing this, they create a formal deductive system.
<br/>
This process is a bit like walking into a massive library and reorganizing all the books based strictly on their physical height, totally ignoring the actual stories printed inside them. We are focusing purely on the physical structure.
<br/>
Now, if we want this empty, abstract structure to actually work, it requires a few essential traits. Let's break the big ones down.
<br/>
First is consistency. This is absolutely critical. Your system cannot contain two formulas that contradict each other. Why is that a big deal? Imagine signing a rental lease where clause A states that pets are completely banned, while clause B states that you must own a cat. The entire contract becomes functionally useless. In logic, if your system contains a contradiction, the whole universe of rules unravels. From a contradiction, literally any statement can be supposedly "proven."
<br/>
Second is independence. This simply means none of your starting axioms can be deduced from the others. It is not exactly a tragedy if your axioms are not independent. It is mostly just considered ugly. It is like printing out three copies of the exact same driving directions for a road trip. It works, but it is deeply redundant. You really only want the bare minimum number of rules to run your system.
<br/>
Science is organized knowledge, not a pile of disconnected facts
Third is completeness. Basically, a system is complete if every single valid statement that exists within your structure can be definitively proven or disproven by your rules.
<br/>
We are getting closer to pure logical rigor here, but there is still a slight catch. Even when using blank symbols, logicians might still unconsciously rely on the normal, everyday meanings of connective words like "and," "or," and "not."
<br/>
How do we remove that final bit of bias? We go one level deeper. We construct what is called a logistic system.
<br/>
A logistic system entirely ignores meaning. It gives you an alphabet of blank symbols and incredibly strict syntax rules for stringing them together. Think of syntax as an approved mechanical pattern. You know how a jigsaw puzzle piece with a round tab can physically only slot into a piece with a round hole? The pieces do not know or care about the colorful picture painted on top of them. They only care about matching physical shapes.
<br/>
By filtering strings of symbols through these strict syntax patterns, a logistic system creates a pile of acceptable formulas known as [Well-Formed Formulas](/logic-modules/module10-propositional-calculus). Once we have carefully established this totally flawless structure, only then do we start injecting actual meaning back into the symbols, which is a final step we call semantics.
<br/>
In other words, are we basically saying that rigorous science boils down to an incredibly strict puzzle of rearranging empty shapes? Yes, that is exactly the secret. By totally isolating the underlying structural rules from the messy meanings of words, logicians build a beautifully sturdy foundation capable of holding the full weight of human knowledge.
